## Moving to a non-profit foundation

In August 2024, the [Open Home Foundation took over](/blog/2024/08/08/works-with-home-assistant-becomes-part-ohf/) Works with Home Assistant. ## It‚Äôs all about the devices  

When we started the program we certified brands, but now we certify devices. Each certified device has to work **locally**, without the need for cloud subscriptions or control.

We can now certify in phases, rather than overwhelming our testers with a truckload of devices in order to launch one partner. ## What can be controlled in Home Assistant

A core aim of the program is to ensure all certified devices have their ‚Äúkey functionality‚Äù available within Home Assistant. So how do we decide what aspects are controllable in Home Assistant and what doesn't make the cut?

1. Key: First, we look at the functionality as a whole. Let‚Äôs use a door lock for example. The door should lock and unlock from within Home Assistant. That‚Äôs *key* functionality, get it? üòâ  
2. Secondary: If the lock also chimes when it locks or unlocks, we think of that as ‚Äúsecondary‚Äù functionality. We recommend that the manufacturer has it as an ‚Äúexposed feature‚Äù in Home Assistant, so you can turn it off during quiet hours for example, but it wouldn‚Äôt block certification.  
3. We have to look at what‚Äôs actually supported by the open standard that we‚Äôre testing against too. If a feature is not currently supported by the specification, there‚Äôs no way for the manufacturer to actually implement it. This is one of the major challenges in certifying against ‚Äòyounger‚Äô specifications such as Matter.

We use our best judgment on this, but we also want your feedback, because everyone has a slightly different point of view, even within our team and testers ‚Äì so look out for our user research requests, or please share your thoughts in our comments below!
